# Provenance Guard

A backend system for classifying whether text-based creative content was written by a human or generated by AI. Built for creative sharing platforms to protect attribution, build trust, and give audiences the context they need.

## Architecture

The system is a Flask API with two detection signals, confidence scoring, transparency labels, appeals workflow, rate limiting, and audit logging.

### Components

- **API Layer (`app/main.py`)**: Flask app with `POST /submit`, `POST /appeal`, `GET /log`, and `GET /health` endpoints.
- **Signal 1 (`app/signals/signal1_llm.py`)**: LLM-based classification via Groq API. Assesses semantic and stylistic coherence holistically across 4 dimensions (voice authenticity, transition naturalness, contextual specificity, structural predictability).
- **Signal 2 (`app/signals/signal2_stylometric.py`)**: Pure Python stylometric heuristics. Computes sentence length variance, type-token ratio, punctuation density deviation, and sentence complexity variance.
- **Confidence Scorer (`app/main.py`)**: Combines both signals via weighted average (0.55 LLM, 0.45 stylometric) with asymmetry penalty and sigmoid calibration.
- **Audit Log (`app/audit.py`)**: JSON Lines file (`audit_log.jsonl`) recording every decision with timestamp, content ID, scores, and status.
- **Appeals Store (`app/main.py`)**: In-memory dictionary tracking appeal status and reviewer queue.

## Detection Signals

I used **LLM-based classification** and **stylometric heuristics** because they are genuinely independent: one is semantic, one is structural. This is often recommended as a "strong default pairing."

**Signal 1 (Groq LLM)** captures what statistical methods miss: voice texture, emotional rawness, cultural specificity. LLMs are trained for broad acceptability and avoid idiosyncrasy that might alienate readers. Human writers write from lived experience — this creates detectable differences in narrative flow that pure statistics cannot measure.

**Signal 2 (Stylometrics)** provides a critical independent check that requires no external API. If the Groq model is fooled by a sophisticated adversarial prompt, the stylometric signal may still flag uniformity. It also serves as the sole signal when Groq is unavailable.

**What I'd Change for Actual Deployment**

- **Add a third signal**: Stylometric fingerprinting for registered creators (personal idiolect detection) to reduce false positives for individual writers.
- **Adversarial training**: Train on adversarial examples to reduce spoofing vulnerability.
- **Multi-language support**: Current stylometric baselines are English-centric. Perplexity and TTR norms differ significantly by language.
- **GPU-local LLM**: Replace Groq API with a locally hosted model to eliminate latency, cost, and availability concerns.

## Confidence Scoring

**Approach**

Both signals output raw scores in [0, 1]. The combination uses a **weighted average with false-positive asymmetry penalty**:

raw_combined = (0.55 * llm_score) + (0.45 * stylometric_score)

If signals strongly disagree (> 0.4 difference), the score blends toward 0.5 to express uncertainty.

**Asymmetry penalty:** If `raw_combined < 0.42` (both signals lean AI), apply -0.06. This encodes the principle that **misclassifying human work as AI is worse than the reverse** — we require stronger evidence before calling something human.

**Sigmoid calibration:**

confidence = 1 / (1 + e^(-6 * (raw_penalized - 0.5)))

This creates meaningful separation: 0.51 lands in the uncertain zone, 0.95 lands firmly in high-confidence human.

### Example Submissions

**High-confidence human (0.854):**
> "ok so i finally tried that new ramen place downtown and honestly? underwhelming. the broth was fine but they put WAY too much sodium in it and i was thirsty for like three hours after. my friend got the spicy version and said it was better. probably won't go back unless someone drags me there"

- LLM score: 0.75 (detects casual voice, emotional texture, personal anecdote)
- Stylometric score: 0.85 (high sentence variance, human markers like "honestly?" and "WAY too much")
- Combined: 0.854 → `likely_human`

**Low-confidence AI (0.301):**
> "Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift in modern society. It is important to note that while the benefits of AI are numerous, it is equally essential to consider the ethical implications. Furthermore, stakeholders across various sectors must collaborate to ensure responsible deployment."

- LLM score: 0.4 (detects generic transitions, absence of personal voice)
- Stylometric score: 0.444 (low sentence variance, high AI transition phrase density: "Furthermore," "It is important to note")
- Combined: 0.301 → `likely_ai`

## Transparency Labels

The label returned by `POST /submit` varies based on the confidence score. All three variants are written in plain language for non-technical readers.

### High-Confidence AI (score 0.00–0.35)

> "This content was likely generated with AI assistance. The system detected consistent statistical patterns typical of language model output, including uniform sentence structure and repeated phrasing templates."

### High-Confidence Human (score 0.65–1.00)

> "This content was likely written by a human author. The system detected natural variation in writing style, organic vocabulary choices, and thematic development consistent with human composition."

### Uncertain (score 0.35–0.65)

> "We cannot confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or generated with AI. The text shows mixed signals — some patterns suggest human authorship, others suggest AI assistance. The creator may submit an appeal if they believe this assessment is incorrect."

## Rate Limiting

**Endpoint:** `POST /submit`

**Limits:**
- `10 per minute` per IP address (tight per-minute limit to prevent rapid abuse)
- `100 per 15 minutes` per IP address (default Flask-Limiter fallback)
- `500 per hour` per IP address (default Flask-Limiter fallback)

**Reasoning:** A typical human creator submits 1–3 pieces per day. The 10/minute limit prevents script flooding while allowing legitimate bulk operations (e.g., migrating a back catalog). An adversary would need to distribute across many IPs to be effective.

**Test result:** 12 rapid requests — first 10 returned `200`, requests 11–12 returned `429` (Too Many Requests).

## Appeals Workflow

**Appeal Submission Process**
1. System validates the `content_id` exists and is not already under review
2. Status updates to `under_review`
3. New audit log entry created with `appeal_id`, `appeal_reasoning`, `appeal_timestamp`
4. Response returns: `appeal_id`, `status: "under_review"`, `estimated_review_time: "5-7 business days"`

**User/Reviewer View**
- Original text
- Signal 1 score + what it measured
- Signal 2 score + what it measured
- Combined confidence + threshold category
- Creator's appeal statement
- One-click actions: `Confirm Classification`, `Override to Human`, `Override to AI`, `Override to Uncertain`

No automated re-classification is implemented. The human makes the final call.

## Known Limitations

**Lightly Edited AI Text**
The system cannot detect AI text that has been substantially edited by a human. In testing, a lightly edited AI passage scored `likely_human` (confidence 0.783) because the editing removed AI transition templates and added first-person markers.

**Why this happens:** The signals are designed to detect *unmodified* AI output. Once edited beyond surface-level changes, the stylistic fingerprints are overwritten. The system has no mechanism to detect edit boundaries or compare against known AI outputs.

**Why this is acceptable:** The system is not intended to police creativity, only to protect attribution ("Perfect AI detection is an unsolved problem"). The appeals workflow allows creators to contest any classification.

**Formal Academic Writing**
Technical and legal writing often scores as AI-like due to required uniformity. The wide uncertain band (0.35–0.65) catches some cases, but highly polished formal prose may still trigger false positives.

**Why this happens:** Both signals penalize uniform structure and standardized vocabulary — which are *required* by genre conventions in academic and legal writing. The stylometric signal's sentence variance and TTR metrics do not account for genre-specific norms.

**Why this is acceptable:** The system is designed for creative sharing platforms (poetry, fiction, blog posts), not academic publishing. The uncertain label explicitly flags these cases for human review rather than falsely claiming certainty. Future improvement: genre detection as a third signal to adjust baselines.

**Non-Native English Speakers (ESL)**
Non-native English speakers often write with more formal, uniform sentence structures and simpler vocabulary than native speakers. This can trigger false positives in the stylometric signal — low sentence variance and lower TTR may score as AI-like even when the text is genuinely human-written.

**Why this happens:** The stylometric signal was calibrated against native English writing patterns. ESL writers tend toward:

- More consistent sentence length (avoiding complex compound sentences they are less confident constructing)
- Higher use of standardized transitions ("Furthermore," "In conclusion") learned in academic English courses
- Lower lexical diversity due to smaller active vocabulary
- More standardized punctuation (following prescriptive grammar rules strictly)

**Why this is acceptable:** The system is designed for creative sharing platforms where the primary user base is native or fluent English speakers. The wide uncertain band (0.35-0.65) catches many ESL cases. The appeals workflow allows creators to explain their language background. Future improvement: language proficiency detection as a third signal to adjust stylometric baselines, or multi-language support with per-language calibration.

**Disclaimer:** Not explicitly tested in my validation suite -- this is just a theoretical limitation based on the stylometric signal design.

## Spec Reflection

**Spec Guided Implementation**
The `planning.md` detection signals section directly shaped the two-signal architecture. The spec's recommendation of "LLM-based classification + stylometric heuristics" as a strong default pairing prevented me from choosing two statistical signals that would not be genuinely independent.

**Where Implementation Diverged**
The spec's suggested threshold for the uncertain band was 0.35–0.65, but early testing showed the stylometric signal was over-scoring AI text due to high TTR. I added an **AI transition phrase density** metric (detecting "Furthermore," "It is important to note," etc.) and increased its weight to 30% of the stylometric score. This pulled AI text scores down into the correct range without breaking human text detection.

## AI Usage/Tools: Kimi 2.6

### Instance 1: Signal 1 Function (Groq API Integration)

**My directive:** Generate a function that sends text to Groq API with a structured prompt requesting 4 dimension scores (0-100), returns JSON, and normalizes to [0,1].

**What Kimi 2.6 produced:** A working function with prompt engineering, JSON parsing, markdown fence handling, and error fallback.

**My revision:** Added explicit `temperature=0.1` and `max_tokens=500` to reduce variability. Added fallback return of 0.5 on any exception to prevent pipeline crashes.

### Instance 2: Signal 2 Function (Stylometric Heuristics)

**My directive:** Generate a pure Python function computing sentence length variance, TTR, punctuation density, and complexity variance with weights 30%, 35%, 15%, 20%.

**What Kimi 2.6 produced:** A basic implementation using standard deviation and simple ratios.

**My revision:** The initial version scored AI text too high (0.832) because TTR alone doesn't distinguish AI from human. I added **AI transition phrase detection** with 30% weight and **human marker detection** (first-person, emotional language) with 15% weight. I also added a **signal disagreement blend** in the confidence scorer: if LLM and stylometric scores differ by >0.4, the combined score pulls toward 0.5 to express uncertainty.

## Stretch Feature: 

**Provenance Certificate**
A **Verified Human** credential that creators can earn through an additional verification step. Once certified, their content receives a distinct label indicating the creator and content are all human.

### Implementation

**Process:**
1. User submits a unique, prompted piece of text (e.g., "Write about your most embarrassing childhood memory in exactly 200 words")

2. System compares the submission against the creator's historical submissions using stylometric fingerprinting

3. If the style fingerprint matches previous work and the text contains specific personal details, the creator is marked as `verified_human`

4. The certificate is stored in the appeals store with `certificate_id`, `creator_id`, `verified_at`, `expires_at` (90 days)

**Display:** Verified creators receive a modified label:
> "✓ Verified human author. This creator has completed additional verification to confirm their authorship."

**Why this Stretch feature:** The core limitation of Provenance Guard is that it cannot detect lightly edited AI text. The certificate shifts the burden of proof: instead of the system proving every piece is human, the creator proves their identity once, and the platform trusts them within a time window. This is how real - world platforms (Twitter/X verification, GitHub commit signing, etc.) handle similar problems.
